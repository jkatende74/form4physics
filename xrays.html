1. Introduction to X-rays

X-rays are a form of high-energy electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths ranging from approximately 0.01 to 10 nanometers. This places them between ultraviolet (UV) radiation and gamma rays in the electromagnetic spectrum. Their discovery by Wilhelm Conrad Roentgen in 1895 revolutionized medical diagnosis and opened up new avenues in scientific research. This guide will delve into the production, properties, applications, and safety considerations of X-rays as relevant to the KCSE Physics syllabus.

2. Production of X-rays

X-rays are produced when fast-moving electrons are suddenly stopped or decelerated. This process occurs in a specialized vacuum tube called an X-ray tube.

The X-Ray Tube Components and Operation

  • Cathode (Filament): A filament, typically made of tungsten, is heated by a low voltage supply. This heating causes thermionic emission, where electrons are released from the filament's surface. The cathode also includes a focusing cup that helps direct the emitted electrons towards the anode.
  • Anode (Target): The anode is a metal target, often made of tungsten or molybdenum, embedded in a copper block. Tungsten is chosen for its high atomic number (leading to more efficient X-ray production) and high melting point (to withstand the heat generated). The copper block helps to conduct heat away. Some X-ray tubes have rotating anodes to distribute the heat over a larger area.
  • High Voltage Supply (EHT): A very high potential difference (Extra High Tension, typically tens to hundreds of kilovolts) is applied between the cathode and the anode. This accelerates the electrons to very high speeds.
  • Evacuated Glass Envelope: The cathode and anode are enclosed in a highly evacuated glass tube. This vacuum prevents electron collisions with air molecules.
  • Cooling System: Efficient cooling mechanisms (e.g., cooling fins, oil circulation) are essential as over 99% of the electron's kinetic energy is converted to heat at the anode.
  • Lead Shielding: The X-ray tube is housed within a lead-lined casing to absorb stray X-rays.

Process of X-ray Production

  1. The filament (cathode) is heated, and electrons are emitted via thermionic emission.
  2. The high potential difference accelerates these electrons towards the anode at high kinetic energies.
  3. When these high-speed electrons strike the metal target (anode), they are rapidly decelerated. Their kinetic energy is converted into X-ray photons and heat.
  4. Two main processes result in X-ray photon production:
    • Bremsstrahlung (Braking Radiation): Incident electrons are deflected and slowed by the target atom's nucleus. The lost kinetic energy is emitted as an X-ray photon. This produces a continuous spectrum of X-ray energies.
    • Characteristic X-rays: An incident electron knocks out an inner-shell electron from a target atom. An electron from a higher energy level fills the vacancy, emitting an X-ray photon of specific energy characteristic of the target material.

3. Energy Changes in an X-Ray Tube

Several energy transformations occur within an X-ray tube:

  • Electrical energy (low voltage) \(\rightarrow\) Heat energy (in filament for thermionic emission).
  • Electrical potential energy (high voltage) \(\rightarrow\) Kinetic energy (of accelerating electrons).
  • Kinetic energy of electrons (upon impact with anode) \(\rightarrow\) Heat energy (predominantly, >99%) + Electromagnetic energy (X-ray photons, <1\%).

The maximum energy of an X-ray photon (\(E_{max}\)) produced by Bremsstrahlung is limited by the total kinetic energy of the incident electron (\(KE_e\)), which depends on the accelerating voltage (\(V\)). If an electron converts all its kinetic energy into a single X-ray photon:

\[ KE_e = eV \] \[ E_{max} = hf_{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda_{min}} \] Thus, \(eV = hf_{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda_{min}}\)

Where:

  • e = charge of an electron (\(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\) C)
  • V = accelerating potential difference (Volts)
  • h = Planck's constant (\(6.63 \times 10^{-34}\) Js)
  • fmax = maximum frequency of the X-rays produced (Hz)
  • c = speed of light (\(3.0 \times 10^8\) m/s)
  • λmin = minimum wavelength of the X-rays produced (m)

Control of X-ray beam characteristics:

  • Hardness (Penetrating Power): Increased by increasing the accelerating voltage (V or EHT). This results in higher \(f_{max}\) and lower \(\lambda_{min}\).
  • Intensity (Quantity of X-rays): Increased by increasing the tube current (mA), which is controlled by the filament current (heater current). More electrons hitting the target per second result in more X-ray photons.

4. Properties of X-rays

  • Electromagnetic Waves: They are EM radiation with very short wavelengths (0.01 nm to 10 nm) and high frequencies.
  • Travel in Straight Lines: Propagate rectilinearly at the speed of light in a vacuum.
  • Not Deflected by Electric or Magnetic Fields: Being uncharged, they are unaffected by these fields.
  • Penetrating Power: Can pass through materials opaque to visible light. Penetration increases with X-ray energy (hardness) and decreases with material density and atomic number.
  • Differential Absorption: Absorbed to different extents by different materials, forming the basis of X-ray imaging (e.g., bones absorb more than soft tissue).
  • Ionization: They are ionizing radiation, capable of knocking electrons out of atoms/molecules, leading to biological effects.
  • Photographic Effect: Affect photographic film, causing blackening, used for radiographs.
  • Fluorescence: Cause certain materials (e.g., zinc sulphide) to emit visible light. Used in fluoroscopy and intensifying screens.
  • Diffraction and Interference: Can be diffracted by crystals, used in X-ray crystallography.
  • No Charge: X-rays are electrically neutral.

Soft and Hard X-rays

  • Soft X-rays: Longer wavelengths, lower energy, less penetrating. Produced at lower accelerating voltages.
  • Hard X-rays: Shorter wavelengths, higher energy, more penetrating. Produced at higher accelerating voltages. The hardness is controlled by the EHT.

5. Dangers of X-rays and Safety Precautions

The ionizing nature of X-rays makes them hazardous to living tissues.

Dangers of X-ray Exposure:

  • Cell Damage and Mutation: Can damage DNA, leading to mutations, cancer (carcinogenic effects), or cell death.
  • Tissue Burns: High doses can cause skin burns and damage to deeper tissues.
  • Genetic Effects: Damage to reproductive cells can cause mutations passed to offspring.
  • Other Acute Effects: High exposures can cause radiation sickness (nausea, vomiting, etc.).

Safety Precautions (ALARA Principle: As Low As Reasonably Achievable):

  • Minimizing Exposure Time: Reduce the duration of exposure.
  • Maximizing Distance: Intensity decreases with the square of the distance from the source.
  • Shielding: Use absorbing materials like lead aprons, thyroid collars, leaded gloves, lead glass screens, and lead-lined walls.
  • Monitoring Exposure: Radiation workers wear dosimeters (e.g., film badges).
  • Controlled Access: Restricted access to X-ray areas with warning signs.
  • Proper Training: Operators must be trained in safety procedures.
  • Equipment Maintenance: Regular inspection and maintenance of X-ray equipment.
  • Patient Protection: Use minimum necessary exposures, shield sensitive body parts. Pregnant women and children should avoid unnecessary exposure.

6. Uses of X-rays

  • Medical Diagnosis (Radiography):
    • Detecting bone fractures, dental problems (cavities, impacted teeth).
    • Diagnosing lung conditions (pneumonia, tuberculosis, cancer).
    • Identifying tumors, abnormal masses, foreign objects.
    • Mammography (breast cancer screening).
    • Contrast X-rays (using barium meals, iodine agents) to visualize soft tissues like the digestive system, blood vessels (angiography).
    • Computed Tomography (CT) scans for detailed 3D cross-sectional images.
  • Medical Treatment (Radiotherapy):
    • High-energy X-rays are used to kill cancer cells and shrink tumors.
  • Industrial Applications:
    • Non-destructive testing (NDT) to find flaws in metal castings, welds.
    • Inspecting packaged goods.
    • Measuring material thickness.
  • Security:
    • Scanning luggage and cargo at airports and checkpoints.
  • Scientific Research (X-ray Crystallography):
    • Determining atomic/molecular structure of crystals (e.g., proteins, DNA).
  • Astronomy:
    • X-ray telescopes detect X-rays from celestial objects (stars, galaxies, black holes).
  • Art and Archaeology:
    • Examining paintings for underlying layers; analyzing artifacts.

7. Problem Solving (Calculations)

Constants to use: \(e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}\), \(h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34} \text{ Js}\), \(c = 3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}\)

Example 1:

An X-ray tube is operated at a potential difference of 50 kV. Calculate the minimum wavelength of the X-rays produced.

Solution:
Given \(V = 50 \text{ kV} = 50 \times 10^3 \text{ V}\). Using \(\lambda_{min} = \frac{hc}{eV}\):
\(\lambda_{min} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.0 \times 10^8)}{(1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times (50 \times 10^3)} = \frac{19.89 \times 10^{-26}}{8.0 \times 10^{-15}} = 2.48625 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m} = 0.0249 \text{ nm}\)

Example 2:

The minimum wavelength of X-rays produced by an X-ray tube is $0.05 \text{ nm}$. Calculate the accelerating potential difference applied to the tube.

Solution:
Given \(\lambda_{min} = 0.05 \text{ nm} = 0.05 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}\). Using \(V = \frac{hc}{e\lambda_{min}}\):
\(V = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.0 \times 10^8)}{(1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times (0.05 \times 10^{-9})} = \frac{19.89 \times 10^{-26}}{0.08 \times 10^{-28}} = 2.48625 \times 10^4 \text{ V} = 24.86 \text{ kV}\)

Example 3:

An X-ray tube operates with an accelerating potential of $60 \text{ kV}$. Calculate the maximum frequency of the X-rays produced.

Solution:
Given \(V = 60 \text{ kV} = 60 \times 10^3 \text{ V}\). Using \(f_{max} = \frac{eV}{h}\):
\(f_{max} = \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times (60 \times 10^3)}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}} = \frac{9.6 \times 10^{-15}}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 1.45 \times 10^{19} \text{ Hz}\)

Example 4:

An X-ray tube operates at $40 \text{ kV}$ and the current flowing through it is $10 \text{ mA}$.
(a) How many electrons strike the target per second?
(b) Calculate the kinetic energy gained by each electron.
(c) What is the total kinetic energy of the electrons striking the target per second (input power)?

Solution:
Given \(V = 40 \text{ kV} = 40 \times 10^3 \text{ V}\), \(I = 10 \text{ mA} = 10 \times 10^{-3} \text{ A}\).
(a) Number of electrons/sec (\(n_e/t\)) = \(I/e = (10 \times 10^{-3}) / (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) = 6.25 \times 10^{16}\) electrons/s.
(b) KE per electron = \(eV = (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times (40 \times 10^3) = 6.4 \times 10^{-15}\) J.
(c) Total KE/sec (Power) = \(IV = (10 \times 10^{-3}) \times (40 \times 10^3) = 400\) W. (Also, Total KE/sec = (electrons/sec) \(\times\) (KE/electron) = \((6.25 \times 10^{16}) \times (6.4 \times 10^{-15}) = 400\) W).
