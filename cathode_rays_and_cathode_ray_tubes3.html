Section 1: Introduction

The study of cathode rays is a crucial part of physics that significantly changed our understanding of matter and electricity. Cathode rays are invisible rays that originate from the negative electrode (cathode) in evacuated tubes. They were discovered to be streams of electrons. This discovery led to important technological developments, such as early display devices and scientific instruments. Chapter 7 of the KLB Physics Book 4 covers this topic on pages 131 to 142. Topic 37.0.0 of the KCSE Physics Syllabus is dedicated to "CATHODE RAYS AND CATHODE RAY TUBE". Understanding cathode rays is foundational for later topics in modern physics like X-rays and the Photoelectric Effect, both of which involve electron behavior.

Section 2: Production of Cathode Rays

KCSE Syllabus Alignment: Objective 37.1.0 (a) describe the production of cathode rays; Content 37.2.1 Production of cathode rays, cathode ray tube.

Cathode rays are primarily generated through a process called thermionic emission within a specialized vacuum tube known as a cathode ray tube (CRT).

Thermionic Emission

This phenomenon involves the liberation of electrons from the surface of a material, typically a metal or coated metal, when it is heated to a sufficiently high temperature. Electrons are normally bound to atoms by attractive forces, and they need to acquire enough energy, called the work function, to escape the surface. Heating provides the necessary thermal energy. When the thermal energy equals or exceeds the work function, electrons are emitted.

Experimental Setup for Thermionic Emission

A typical setup (as illustrated in Figure 7.1 of KLB Physics Book 4) involves:

  • An evacuated glass bulb to prevent electron collisions with air molecules.
  • A cathode, which is the electron-emitting electrode. It is often coated with materials like barium and strontium oxides because they have a relatively low work function, allowing electron emission at lower temperatures.
  • A heater filament which heats the cathode, either directly or indirectly, driven by a low voltage.
  • An anode maintained at a positive potential relative to the cathode.
  • Power supplies for the heater and for the potential difference between the anode and cathode.
Placeholder for Diagram: Experimental Setup for Thermionic Emission (similar to KLB Fig 7.1)

When the cathode is heated sufficiently, it emits electrons. If the anode is positive, these electrons are attracted to it, creating a current flow that can be measured. The magnitude of the heater current affects the cathode temperature and thus the rate of thermionic emission, which in turn controls the electron beam current and screen brightness in CRTs.

Basic Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) for Production

A simple CRT designed for producing cathode rays (as illustrated in Figure 7.2 of KLB Physics Book 4) includes:

  • Heated Cathode: The source of electrons via thermionic emission.
  • Anode: Maintained at a very high positive potential using an Extra High Tension (EHT) supply to accelerate the electrons.
  • Evacuated Glass Tube: Essential for preventing collisions between accelerated electrons and air molecules, which would cause energy loss, scattering, and potential ionization of gas molecules. The vacuum ensures electrons travel unimpeded.
  • Fluorescent Screen: The end of the tube coated with a fluorescent material (e.g., zinc sulphide) that emits light when struck by the accelerated electrons, making the path of the cathode rays visible as a glowing spot or pattern.
Placeholder for Diagram: Basic Cathode Ray Tube for Production (similar to KLB Fig 7.2)

In operation, electrons from the heated cathode are accelerated to high velocities by the electric field between the anode and cathode, forming the cathode rays.

Section 3: Properties of Cathode Rays

KCSE Syllabus Alignment: Objective 37.1.0 (b) state the properties of cathode rays; Content 37.2.2 Properties of cathode rays.

Cathode rays possess several distinct properties that helped identify them as streams of fast-moving electrons. These properties are detailed on pages 132-133 of the KLB Physics Book 4.

  • Travel in Straight Lines: In the absence of external electric or magnetic fields, cathode rays move in straight lines. This is demonstrated by placing an opaque object, like a Maltese cross, in the beam's path, which casts a sharp shadow on the screen. This property is important for focused beams in devices like oscilloscopes and TV tubes.
    Placeholder for Diagram: Maltese Cross Experiment (similar to KLB Fig 7.3)
  • Cause Fluorescence: Cathode rays can cause certain materials (phosphors like zinc sulphide) to emit visible light when struck. This property is used in CRT screens to visualize the beam.
  • Are Negatively Charged: Cathode rays consist of negatively charged particles.
    • In an electric field, the beam is deflected towards the positive plate.
      Placeholder for Diagram: Deflection in Electric Field (similar to KLB Fig 7.4a)
    • In a magnetic field, the beam is deflected according to Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (considering electron flow).
      Placeholder for Diagram: Deflection in Magnetic Field (similar to KLB Fig 7.4b)
    These deflections confirm their negative charge. The direction of deflection allowed scientists like J.J. Thomson to determine the charge-to-mass ratio (\(e/m\)) and identify them as electrons.
  • Possess Kinetic Energy: Cathode rays, being particles with mass moving at high velocities, have kinetic energy (\(KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\)). This energy is converted into light when they cause fluorescence and into heat when they strike a target.
  • Produce X-rays: When fast-moving cathode rays (electrons) are suddenly stopped or decelerated by hitting a dense metal target in an X-ray tube, their kinetic energy is converted into X-rays. This is the basis of X-ray generation (covered in Chapter 8).

These properties arise from the fact that cathode rays are electrons. Their particulate nature allows them to gain kinetic energy and be deflected by fields. This energy can be converted into light or X-rays upon interaction with matter.

Section 4: The Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO): Structure and Functioning

KCSE Syllabus Alignment: Objective 37.1.0 (c) explain the functioning of a Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (C.R.O)...; Content 37.2.3 C.R.O... tubes.

The CRO is an advanced instrument based on the CRT, used for displaying, measuring, and analyzing electrical signals, often showing voltage versus time. The main components, shown in Figures 7.5 and 7.6 (KLB p.134), are the electron gun, deflection system, evacuated glass envelope, and fluorescent screen.

Placeholder for Diagram: Main Components of a CRO (similar to KLB Fig 7.5 & 7.6)

4.1 The Electron Gun

Produces a narrow, focused, and accelerated beam of electrons. It includes:

  • Heated Cathode: Source of electrons via thermionic emission, often coated with low work function oxides.
  • Grid (Control Grid): A cylindrical electrode with a negative potential relative to the cathode. It controls the number of electrons in the beam, thus regulating the brightness of the spot on the screen.
  • Anodes (Accelerating and Focusing): Cylindrical electrodes at high positive potentials from an EHT supply via a potential divider (KLB Fig 7.7). They accelerate electrons and form an electrostatic lens system (KLB Fig 7.8) to focus the beam to a sharp spot. The "Focus" knob adjusts the potential difference between these anodes.
    Placeholder for Diagram: Potential Divider for Anodes (similar to KLB Fig 7.7)
    Placeholder for Diagram: Electrostatic Lens System (similar to KLB Fig 7.8)

4.2 The Deflection System

Directs the electron beam across the screen. It has two pairs of plates:

  • Vertical Deflection Plates (Y-plates): Horizontal plates (KLB Fig 7.9). An input signal voltage applied here creates a vertical electric field, deflecting the beam vertically in proportion to the signal's instantaneous magnitude. An AC voltage produces a vertical line if the time base is off.
    Placeholder for Diagram: Y-plates deflection (similar to KLB Fig 7.9)
  • Horizontal Deflection Plates (X-plates): Vertical plates (KLB Fig 7.10). A voltage here causes horizontal deflection.
    Placeholder for Diagram: X-plates deflection (similar to KLB Fig 7.10)
  • Time Base Circuit: Typically applies a sawtooth voltage (KLB Fig 7.11) to the X-plates. This voltage linearly sweeps the beam horizontally across the screen at a constant speed (sweep phase) and then rapidly returns it (flyback phase). The "Time/Div" control adjusts the sweep speed. A fast sweep creates a continuous horizontal line (KLB Fig 7.12).
    Placeholder for Diagram: Sawtooth Voltage Waveform (similar to KLB Fig 7.11)
    Placeholder for Diagram: Horizontal Line from Time Base (similar to KLB Fig 7.12)

The CRO displays waveforms by using the Y-plates for vertical (signal) deflection and the X-plates (with the time base) for horizontal (time) deflection simultaneously. This creates a visual graph of voltage versus time.

4.3 The Fluorescent Screen and Graphite Coating

  • Fluorescent Screen: Coated with phosphor (e.g., zinc sulphide) which emits light when struck by the electron beam, creating a luminous spot. Screen persistence (glowing briefly after the beam moves) and human vision persistence help perceive rapidly changing spots as continuous lines or waveforms.
  • Graphite Coating (Aquadag): A conductive layer on the inner walls of the tube. It collects secondary electrons and primary beam electrons and returns them to earth. It also provides electrostatic shielding from external fields and can act as a post-deflection accelerating electrode.

Section 5: Uses of the Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO)

KCSE Syllabus Alignment: Objective 37.1.0 (d) explain the uses of a Cathode Ray Oscilloscope; Content 37.2.4 Uses of C.R.O.

The CRO is a versatile tool.

5.1 As a Voltmeter

Can measure DC and AC voltages.

  • Procedure: Time base is off, voltage applied to Y-input.
  • Measurement: DC voltage is found by vertical deflection (in divisions) multiplied by the Y-gain setting (V/div). \(Voltage = displacement \times sensitivity\). For AC voltage, a vertical line is seen, and the peak voltage is found by measuring the deflection from the center to the top/bottom and multiplying by the Y-gain.
  • Advantages over Conventional Voltmeters:
    • High Input Impedance: Draws negligible current from the circuit, not altering conditions.
    • Measures Both DC and AC: Can handle both voltage types.
    • Instantaneous Response: Electron beam has low inertia, allowing rapid response to voltage changes.
    • Wide Voltage Range and Safety: Adjustable Y-gain allows measurement of various voltages without risk of damage.

5.2 Displaying Waveforms

Most common use. Shows a voltage vs. time graph of a signal. Allows observation of shape, distortions, and measurement of parameters like amplitude and period.

5.3 Determining Frequency

Convenient for AC signals.

  • Procedure: Apply signal to Y-input, turn on time base, adjust "Time/Div" until clear cycles are visible (KLB Fig 7.14).
    Placeholder for Diagram: Waveform for Frequency Measurement (similar to KLB Fig 7.14)
  • Calculation: Measure the horizontal length (divisions) of one cycle (period, \(T\)). Calculate \(T\) by multiplying this length by the time base setting (s/div). Frequency \(f = \frac{1}{T}\). Accurate frequency measurement depends on correctly reading the time base setting and measuring the period on the screen.

Section 6: Problem Solving Involving the Cathode Ray Oscilloscope

KCSE Syllabus Alignment: Objective 37.1.0 (e) solve problems involving Cathode Ray Oscilloscope; Content 37.2.5 Problems involving C.R.O.

Solving numerical problems involving voltage and frequency calculations from CRO traces is an important skill. These problems use the graticule and the calibrated Y-gain and time base settings.

Placeholder for Diagram: Example CRO Trace for Problem Solving (similar to KLB Fig 7.17)

6.1 Calculating Voltage from CRO Trace

When measuring voltage, the Y-gain setting (sensitivity in Volts/division) is crucial. The formula is: \(Voltage = Vertical~deflection~(divisions) \times Y-gain~(V/div)\).

Worked Example (based on KLB Example 2, p.140):

  • Part (a): A DC voltage applied to the Y-plates causes a deflection of 2.5 divisions. If this DC voltage is 50 V, determine the sensitivity of the Y-gain.
    • \(Sensitivity = \frac{Voltage}{Number~of~divisions} = \frac{50~V}{2.5~div} = 20~V/div\).
  • Part (b): If an AC voltage with a peak value of 40 V is fed onto the Y-plates, and the Y-gain is set to 20 V/div, show what will be observed on the screen (assuming time base is off).
    • \(Peak~deflection = \frac{Peak~Voltage}{Y-gain} = \frac{40~V}{20~V/div} = 2~divisions\). A vertical line of 4 divisions total length will be seen.

6.2 Calculating Frequency from CRO Trace

To calculate frequency, the period of the waveform must first be determined using the time base setting (seconds/division or milliseconds/division). The steps are:

  1. Measure the horizontal length (number of divisions, L) for one complete cycle of the waveform.
  2. Note the time base setting (S, in s/div or ms/div).
  3. Calculate the Period (T) of the waveform: \(T = L \times S\).
  4. Calculate the Frequency (f) of the waveform: \(f = \frac{1}{T}\).

Worked Example (based on KLB Example 1, p.139):

  • Given: The time base control is set at 10 ms/div. One complete cycle of the AC signal displayed covers 8 horizontal divisions. Determine the frequency.
    • Period \(T = 8~div \times 10~ms/div = 80~ms = 0.08~s\).
    • Frequency \(f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.08~s} = 12.5~Hz\).

6.3 Combined Problems (Voltage and Frequency)

Worked Example (based on KLB Example 3, p.141):

Figure 7.17 shows a trace on a CRO screen. The time base control is set at 5 ms/div, and the Y-gain is at 100 V/div. The trace shows 2 complete cycles covering 8 horizontal divisions. The peak vertical deflection from the zero level is 3 divisions. Determine: (a) the frequency of the AC signal, and (b) the peak voltage of the input signal.

  • (a) Frequency of the AC signal:
    • Time for 2 cycles = \(8~div \times 5~ms/div = 40~ms\).
    • Period T (time for 1 cycle) = \(\frac{40~ms}{2} = 20~ms = 0.02~s\).
    • Frequency \(f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.02~s} = 50~Hz\).
  • (b) Peak voltage of the input signal:
    • Peak Voltage = Peak deflection \(\times\) Y-gain = \(3~div \times 100~V/div = 300~V\).

Section 7: The Television (TV) Tube

KCSE Syllabus Alignment: Objective 37.1.0 (c)... explain the functioning of a... Television tube (TV tube); Content 37.2.3... TV tubes.

The TV tube is a modified CRT specifically for displaying moving images. It shares the basic principle of an electron gun and fluorescent screen but has significant differences.

Placeholder for Diagram: Structure of a Television Tube (similar to KLB Fig 7.18)

Key Modifications from a Standard CRO:

  • Magnetic Deflection Coils: Unlike the electrostatic plates in a typical CRO, TV tubes use magnetic deflection coils around the tube's neck. These coils, carrying currents, create magnetic fields that deflect the electron beam. Magnetic deflection is more effective for large deflection angles and high-energy beams, allowing for wider screens with shorter tubes.
  • Modulation of Beam Intensity by Video Signal: The incoming video signal is applied to the control grid of the electron gun. As the electron beam rapidly scans the screen in a raster scan pattern (series of horizontal lines), the video signal's voltage changes the grid's potential, modulating the beam's intensity (number of electrons). A stronger signal (bright part of the image) leads to higher beam intensity and a brighter spot; a weaker signal (dark part) leads to lower intensity and a dimmer spot.
  • Image Formation: The rapid variation in brightness, synchronized with the scanning motion, "paints" the image on the screen. High scanning speed and persistence of vision/screen persistence create the illusion of a continuous moving picture.
  • Color TV Tube: More complex, using three electron guns (for Red, Green, Blue) and a screen with corresponding phosphor dots. Varying the intensity of each beam creates different colors through additive mixing.

The TV tube is an example of how the basic CRT has been developed into a complex technology for visual display by precisely controlling the electron beam's position and intensity.

Section 9: Revision Questions

Cathode rays are described as invisible rays emanating from the negative electrode (cathode) in evacuated tubes. They are streams of electrons moving from the cathode to the anode.

Cathode rays originate from the negative electrode (cathode) inside an evacuated tube.

The primary physical phenomenon responsible is thermionic emission.

Thermionic emission is the phenomenon where electrons are liberated from the surface of a material (typically a metal or coated metal) when it is heated to a sufficiently high temperature.

Heating the cathode material provides thermal energy to the electrons within the material. When this thermal energy is equal to or greater than the material's work function, the electrons can escape from the surface. A higher temperature leads to a greater rate of electron emission.

These materials are used because they have a relatively low work function, meaning they emit electrons at lower temperatures than pure metals.

The work function is the minimum energy an electron must acquire to overcome the attractive forces binding it to the parent atom and escape the surface of the material. Heating provides the energy to overcome this barrier.

The essential components include an evacuated glass bulb, a cathode (coated with materials like barium/strontium oxides), a heater filament driven by a low voltage supply (e.g., 6V), an anode at a positive potential relative to the cathode, and power supplies for both the heater and the anode. A milliammeter is used to register the resulting current flow.

The main function of the anode is to accelerate the thermionically emitted electrons towards the other end of the tube by being maintained at a very high positive potential relative to the cathode.

An Extra High Tension (EHT) power supply is used. Its purpose is to create a strong electric field that accelerates the electrons to very high velocities.

A high vacuum is essential to prevent the emitted electrons from colliding with air molecules.

Two negative consequences are: The electrons would lose kinetic energy due to collisions. The electrons would be scattered in various directions, preventing the formation of a focused beam. Air molecules might be ionized, creating charged particles that interfere with the tube's operation.

The primary role is to make the invisible cathode rays visible. When the electrons strike the screen, it emits light (fluoresces), creating a visible spot or pattern.

An example of a material used is zinc sulphide.

In the absence of external fields, cathode rays travel in straight lines.

The Maltese cross experiment involves placing an opaque object (the Maltese cross) in the path of the cathode rays. It casts a sharp, well-defined shadow on the fluorescent screen. The conclusion is that cathode rays propagate in straight lines.

The property utilized is their ability to cause fluorescence.

When cathode rays pass between two charged plates, they are deflected towards the positively charged plate. This attraction to the positive plate is evidence that they carry a negative charge.

The rays will be deflected towards the top plate.

The direction of deflection in a magnetic field is consistent with the force exerted on negatively charged particles moving in that field.

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule is applicable.

It indicates that they possess kinetic energy.

X-rays are produced when cathode rays (fast-moving electrons) are suddenly stopped or decelerated by impacting a dense metal target (an anticathode or target). Their kinetic energy is converted into X-rays.

The instrument is the Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO).

The four main components are: An electron gun. A system of plates for deflecting the electron beam (deflection system). An evacuated strong glass envelope. A fluorescent screen at one end of the glass envelope.

The primary function of the electron gun is to produce a narrow, focused, and accelerated beam of electrons.

The control grid is a cylindrical electrode surrounding the cathode. It is maintained at a negative potential relative to the cathode. Its function is to control the flow of electrons (and thus the intensity of the beam) passing through its aperture. A more negative potential repels more electrons, reducing the beam current and brightness; a less negative potential allows more electrons through, increasing brightness.

Adjusting the brightness knob controls the negative potential of the control grid. By varying this potential, it regulates the number of electrons reaching the screen, thus controlling the intensity (brightness) of the spot.

The two main functions are acceleration of the electrons to high velocities and focusing the electron beam into a sharp spot.

The anodes, particularly those at different potentials (like A1 and A2), create an electric field configuration that acts as an "electrostatic lens" or "electronic lens" to converge the electron beam.

The purpose of the deflection system is to manipulate the path of the electron beam, allowing it to be directed to any point on the screen.

The Y-plates are a pair of horizontal plates. Their function is to provide vertical deflection of the electron beam. The input signal (voltage to be displayed) is typically applied across them.

A vertical line would be observed.

The function of the X-plates (a pair of vertical plates) is to provide horizontal deflection of the electron beam when a voltage is applied across them.

The time base circuit generates a special voltage (a sawtooth waveform) that is applied to the X-plates. This voltage causes the electron beam to sweep horizontally across the screen at a constant speed, providing the time axis for the display.

A sawtooth voltage waveform is generated and applied.

"Sweep" refers to the phase where the sawtooth voltage increases linearly with time, causing the electron beam to move horizontally across the screen from left to right at a constant speed. "Flyback" refers to the rapid drop of the sawtooth voltage to its initial value, causing the beam to quickly return to the left side of the screen to start the next sweep.

The Y-plates provide vertical deflection proportional to the instantaneous signal voltage, while the time base on the X-plates provides horizontal deflection proportional to time. The combination of these simultaneous movements traces the waveform of the input signal on the screen, showing voltage on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis.

Persistence is the property of the fluorescent screen where it continues to glow for a short duration (around 0.05 seconds) after the electron beam has moved past a point. It is important because, combined with human persistence of vision, it allows rapidly changing spots or fast sweeps to be perceived as continuous lines or waveforms, enabling the visualization of dynamic signals.

The conductive coating is called graphite or Aquadag. Two purposes it serves are: It collects secondary electrons emitted from the screen and conducts them, along with the primary beam electrons, back to the power supply (earth), preventing charge build-up on the screen. It shields the electron beam from external stray electric fields that could distort its path. It can act as a post-deflection accelerating electrode.

Two advantages are: High input impedance: The CRO draws negligible current from the circuit, not affecting the circuit conditions. Can measure both DC and AC voltages (including peak/peak-to-peak for AC). Instantaneous response due to low inertia of the electron beam. Wide voltage range measurement with less risk of damage.

With the time base off and an AC signal on the Y-input, a vertical line is displayed. The length of this line corresponds to twice the peak voltage. Measure the vertical displacement from the center (zero-volt level) to the maximum extent of the line in divisions. Multiply this vertical displacement (in divisions) by the Y-gain setting (in V/div) to find the peak voltage.

The steps are: Apply the AC signal to the Y-input and switch on the time base. Adjust the Time/Div control until a few complete, stable cycles are clearly displayed. Carefully measure the horizontal length (number of divisions) corresponding to one complete cycle of the waveform from the screen's graticule. Note the time base setting (e.g., in ms/div or s/div). Calculate the period (T) of the waveform by multiplying the number of divisions for one cycle by the time base setting (T = number of divisions time base setting). Convert the period to seconds if necessary. Calculate the frequency (f) using the relationship \(f = 1/T\).

Period T = (number of divisions for one cycle) (time base setting) = 5 divisions 10 ms/div = 50 ms.

Convert period to seconds: T = 50 ms = 0.05 s. Frequency \(f = 1 / T = 1 / 0.05 s = 20 Hz\).

Voltage = vertical displacement Y-gain = 4 divisions 5 V/div = 20 V.

A standard CRO typically uses electrostatic deflection plates (X and Y plates), while a television tube employs magnetic deflection coils.

Magnetic deflection systems are generally more effective for achieving large deflection angles with high-energy electron beams. This allows TV tubes to have wider screens while maintaining a relatively shorter tube length.

The incoming video signal is applied to the control grid of the electron gun. As the electron beam scans the screen, the instantaneous voltage of the video signal varies the potential of the grid, which in turn modulates the intensity (number of electrons) of the electron beam. This causes the screen to glow with varying brightness at each point, creating the image.

A color TV tube typically uses three separate electron guns (for Red, Green, and Blue). The screen is coated with tiny phosphor dots that emit red, green, or blue light when struck by electrons. By varying the intensities of the three electron beams, the brightness of the respective color phosphors is controlled at each point, and a full spectrum of colors is reproduced through additive color mixing.
