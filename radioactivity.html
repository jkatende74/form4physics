1. Introduction

1.1 Context of Radioactivity in Physics

The discovery of radioactivity in 1896 by Henri Becquerel, followed by the pioneering investigations of Marie and Pierre Curie, marked a watershed moment in the history of science. These scientists observed that certain materials, like uranium compounds, spontaneously emitted penetrating radiations. Their work revealed that these emissions originated from the atomic nucleus, a concept that fundamentally altered the then-prevailing understanding of the atom as an indivisible entity. Radioactivity, therefore, is a natural phenomenon characterized by the spontaneous transformation of unstable atomic nuclei, accompanied by the release of energy and particles. This field of study paved the way for the development of nuclear physics and has had profound implications across various scientific and technological domains.

3. Atomic Structure and Nuclear Stability

Fundamental concepts include protons, neutrons, atomic number (Z), mass number (A), and nuclide notation \({}^{A}_{Z}X\). Isotopes (e.g., Carbon-12, Carbon-14) are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.

Nuclear stability depends on the balance of forces within the nucleus. Stable nuclides often have a proton-to-neutron ratio near 1:1 for lighter elements, but this ratio increases for heavier stable nuclei. The "Stability Curve" (N vs. Z graph) illustrates this.

Unstable nuclides decay to achieve a more stable configuration. This instability arises from an imbalance between the attractive strong nuclear force and the repulsive electrostatic force between protons.

5. Radioactivity and Radioactive Decay

Radioactivity is the spontaneous random emission of particles from the nucleus of an unstable nuclide. It is unaffected by external factors.

Radioactive decay is the process by which an unstable nucleus transforms by emitting radiation.

Types of Decay:

  • Alpha Decay: Emission of an \(\alpha\) particle (\({}_{2}^{4}He\)).

    \({}_{Z}^{A}X \rightarrow {}_{Z-2}^{A-4}Y + {}_{2}^{4}He\)

    Example: \({}_{92}^{238}U \rightarrow {}_{90}^{234}Th + {}_{2}^{4}He\)
  • Beta Decay: Emission of a \(\beta\) particle (\({}_{-1}^{0}e\)). A neutron in the nucleus converts to a proton and an electron (the \(\beta\) particle).

    \({}_{Z}^{A}X \rightarrow {}_{Z+1}^{A}Y + {}_{-1}^{0}e \quad (+ \bar{\nu_e} \text{ antineutrino})\)

    Example: \({}_{11}^{24}Na \rightarrow {}_{12}^{24}Mg + {}_{-1}^{0}e\)
  • Gamma Emission: Emission of a \(\gamma\) ray (photon) when an excited nucleus transitions to a lower energy state. No change in A or Z.

    \({}_{Z}^{A}X^* \rightarrow {}_{Z}^{A}X + \gamma\)

    Example: \({}_{27}^{60}Co^* \rightarrow {}_{27}^{60}Co + \gamma\)

7. Decay Law and Half-Life

Radioactive decay is spontaneous and random.

Decay Law: The rate of disintegration (\(\frac{dN}{dt}\)) is proportional to the number of undecayed nuclides (N) present: \[\frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N\] Where \(\lambda\) is the decay constant (probability of decay per unit time). \(\frac{dN}{dt}\) is also the activity (A) of the sample, so \(A = \lambda N\). The SI unit of activity is the Becquerel (Bq: 1 decay/second).

Half-Life (\(t_{1/2}\)): The time taken for half the number of nuclides initially present in a sample to decay.

Formula for number of undecayed nuclides (N) after time (T): \[N = N_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{\frac{T}{t_{1/2}}}\] Where \(N_0\) is the initial number of nuclides.

Relationship between half-life and decay constant: \[t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda}\]

8. Applications of Radioactivity

  • Carbon Dating: Determining age of organic materials using Carbon-14 (half-life ~5730 years).
  • Medicine:
    • Cancer Treatment (Radiotherapy): Gamma rays (e.g., from Cobalt-60) destroy cancerous cells.
    • Sterilization: Gamma rays sterilize medical equipment.
    • Medical Tracers & Imaging: E.g., Iodine-131 for thyroid function, PET scans.
  • Industrial Applications:
    • Detecting Pipe Leakages: Radioactive tracers added to fluids.
    • Thickness Gauging: Beta source and detector measure material thickness (e.g., paper, metal foil - KLB Fig 10.15).
      Diagram: Thickness gauging setup. (Similar to KLB Fig 10.15)
    • Detection of Flaws: Gamma radiography for inspecting welds.
    • Smoke Detectors: E.g., Americium-241 (alpha emitter).
  • Trace Elements in Research: Studying uptake of fertilizers by plants, water movement in soil.

Choice of radioisotope depends on half-life, type/energy of radiation, and chemical properties.

9. Hazards of Radiation and Safety Precautions

Radiation can damage living cells and tissues. Effects depend on radiation type, dose, and irradiated body part.

Hazards:

  • Cell damage, mutations, cancer (e.g., leukaemia).
  • Skin burns, blisters.
  • Hereditary defects.
  • High doses can be fatal.

Gamma rays are a major external hazard due to high penetration. Alpha emitters are dangerous if ingested/inhaled (internal hazard).

Safety Precautions (ALARA: As Low As Reasonably Achievable):

  1. Time: Minimize exposure duration.
  2. Distance: Maximize distance from the source (intensity \(\propto 1/d^2\)).
  3. Shielding: Use absorbing materials (e.g., lead for \(\gamma\), Perspex for \(\beta\)).
  • Never handle sources with bare hands; use forceps/tongs.
  • Store in thick, dense (e.g., lead) containers.
  • Wear monitoring badges (dosimeters).

10. Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Nuclear Fission

Splitting of a heavy nucleus (e.g., Uranium-235 by neutron bombardment) into lighter nuclei, releasing energy and more neutrons.

\({}_{92}^{235}U + {}_{0}^{1}n \rightarrow {}_{92}^{236}U^* \rightarrow {}_{56}^{144}Ba + {}_{36}^{90}Kr + 2 \text{ or } 3 {}_{0}^{1}n + \text{Energy}\)

Chain Reaction: Neutrons from fission cause further fissions. Controlled in reactors (e.g., using boron rods to absorb neutrons), uncontrolled in atomic bombs.

Diagram: Nuclear fission chain reaction. (Similar to KLB Fig 10.16)

Nuclear Fusion

Combining (fusing) of light nuclei (e.g., hydrogen isotopes) to form a heavier nucleus, releasing substantial energy.

\({}_{1}^{2}H + {}_{1}^{3}H \rightarrow {}_{2}^{4}He + {}_{0}^{1}n + \text{Energy (Deuterium-Tritium fusion)}\)

Requires extremely high temperatures and pressures (occurs in stars). Controlled fusion for power is a research goal.

Energy is released in both because products are more stable (higher binding energy per nucleon).

11. Elaboration on Key Radioactivity Concepts

Probabilistic Nature of Decay

Radioactive decay is spontaneous and random for individual nuclei. The decay constant (\(\lambda\)) is the probability per unit time of decay for a nucleus. For large numbers of nuclei, the decay rate is predictable.

Interaction of Radiation with Matter

  • Alpha: Strong electrostatic interactions, dense ionization, short straight tracks.
  • Beta: Less intense interactions than alpha, longer tortuous tracks.
  • Gamma: Interact via photoelectric effect, Compton scattering, pair production; sparse, indirect ionization, highly penetrating.

Activity, Decay Constant, Half-Life Relationship

Activity \(A = \lambda N\). Half-life \(t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda}\).

Detector Principles & Limitations

G-M Tube: High efficiency for \(\beta\), low for \(\gamma\). No energy info. Dead time limits high count rates.

Cloud Chamber: Visualizes tracks, gives qualitative info on particle type, charge, energy.

Nuclear Energy: Fission vs. Fusion In-depth

Energy release is due to increased binding energy per nucleon in products. Fusion releases more energy per unit mass. Fusion power faces challenges of plasma confinement at extreme temperatures.

Modern Applications & Ethical Considerations

PET scans, smoke detectors. Ethical issues: nuclear waste, accidents, weapons proliferation. ALARA principle is key.

II. Atomic Structure and Nuclear Stability

Atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.

Mass number (A) is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.

\(X\) is the chemical symbol of the element, \(A\) is the mass number (top left), and \(Z\) is the atomic number (bottom left).

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same atomic number Z) that have different numbers of neutrons, and therefore different mass numbers (A).

Carbon-12 (\({}^{12}_{6}C\)) and Carbon-14 (\({}^{14}_{6}C\)).

In light stable nuclei, the proton-to-neutron ratio is approximately 1:1.

For heavier nuclei, stability often requires more neutrons than protons; unstable heavy nuclei may have an excess or deficit of neutrons relative to the stable ratio for their mass.

They tend to decay by beta-minus (\(\beta^-\)) emission, which converts a neutron into a proton, decreasing the neutron number and increasing the proton number, moving them closer to the stability line.

They tend to decay by processes that decrease the proton number relative to the neutron number, such as positron (\(\beta^+\)) emission or electron capture.

The strong nuclear force (attractive) and the electrostatic force (repulsive between protons).

III. Types of Radiations and their Properties

Alpha (\(\alpha\)), Beta (\(\beta\)), and Gamma (\(\gamma\)) radiations.

Alpha particles are deflected as positively charged particles, beta particles as negatively charged particles (deflected more than alpha), and gamma rays are undeflected.

An alpha particle is a helium nucleus, consisting of 2 protons and 2 neutrons. Notation: \({}_{2}^{4}He\) or \(\alpha\).

A beta particle is a high-speed electron. Notation: \({}_{-1}^{0}e\) or \(\beta^-\).

Gamma radiation is high-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons).

Alpha particles have low penetrating power; they can be stopped by a sheet of paper or a few centimeters of air.

Beta particles are more penetrating than alpha particles; they can be stopped by a few millimeters of aluminum.

Gamma rays are highly penetrating; significant thicknesses of lead or concrete are needed for substantial attenuation.

Alpha particles have the highest ionising effect (dense ionization). Beta particles have a moderate ionising effect. Gamma rays have the lowest ionising effect (sparse, indirect ionization).

Due to their high ionisation density (causing thick tracks), high mass (preventing easy deflection, hence straight tracks), and rapid energy loss (short tracks).

Due to their lower ionisation density (thinner tracks), greater penetration (longer tracks), and smaller mass (easily scattered, hence irregular tracks).

Gamma rays are uncharged and ionize indirectly by ejecting electrons from air molecules; these secondary electrons then create the observed weak, sparse tracks.

VI. Radiation Detectors

Radiation affects the emulsion, causing it to darken upon exposure and development. The degree of darkening indicates the dose.

It relies on the condensation of a supersaturated vapour onto ions created by the passage of radiation, forming visible tracks.

Expansion cloud chamber and diffusion cloud chamber.

A gas-filled tube (e.g., argon with bromine), a central wire anode, a cylindrical cathode (tube wall), and a thin mica window.

Electrons produced by initial ionization are accelerated and cause further ionizations by collision, leading to an avalanche of electrons for each detected particle.

A short period after detecting a particle during which the tube is insensitive to further radiation.

To absorb the kinetic energy of positive ions and prevent them from causing secondary electron emission from the cathode, which would lead to false pulses.

The combining or fusing of two light atomic nuclei to form a heavier nucleus, accompanied by the release of substantial energy.
